What if language becomes a currency?

There are many costs that come with language as a currency.
In considering these costs, it is important to refer back to the theories of Ludwig Wittgenstein. As he argues that language is formed through shared practices, language is fundamentally social and public. Privacy, then, is impossible because this meaning relies on shared understandings. Technology challenges these ideologies as it is not fundamentally a freely shared enterprise. There will always be demographics that have prioritized access to advanced innovation. Thus comes the exploration of "What if language becomes a currency?"

Language as a Currency

In considering this future, there are a few notable outcomes:

  • Economic Value of Language.

    • If language becomes a currency, its value would be determined by factors such as the ability to convey complex ideas. People who possess better communication skills, and greater resources to acquire these skills, might have a distinct advantage in economic transactions.

  • Inequality and Access.

    • The concept of language as a currency could exacerbate existing inequalities. People who have access to education and resources for learning languages might have a significant advantage over those who do not.

  • Shifts in Education Emphasis.

    • Education systems might shift their emphasis to prioritize language skills, and linguistic curricula could become a core part of education. Those with better access to these systems will again be at advantage.

  • Language Exchange Markets.

    • Similar to foreign exchange markets where currencies are traded, there could be markets for exchanging different languages. The value of a language might fluctuate based on demand and supply, as well as the perceived economic or cultural importance of that language. In turn, this cultural importance raises question to the relevance and "economic value" attached to different languages. This could lead to conflicts regarding cultural preservation.
